An exploration of HHS data, 2015–2023

Alone in America

More than half a million children crossed the U.S. border without a parent. Where did they end up? And with whom?

Scroll to begin

The Data

In 2023, The New York Times obtained records on every unaccompanied child migrant released by the Office of Refugee Resettlement between January 2015 and May 2023 representing more than 550,000 children.

The records reveal where each child came from, how long they were held, who claimed them, and the ZIP code where they were sent to live.

What follows is a visual journey through that data.

The Journey Begins

Hundreds of thousands of unaccompanied children made the journey north from countries across Central America and Mexico — fleeing poverty, gang violence, and family separation.

Each dot ≈ 100 children.

The Northern Triangle

The vast majority came from just three countries: Honduras, Guatemala, and El Salvador — together known as the Northern Triangle.

Origins

Guatemala sent the most children, followed closely by Honduras. El Salvador accounts for most of the rest.

Mexico — the center of so much political rhetoric about border crossings — accounts for one of the smallest shares. The crisis was overwhelmingly a Central American one, not a Mexican one.

The Journey North

After crossing the border and entering government custody, children were processed and eventually released to sponsors across the United States.

Where They Landed

Children were placed in communities in all 50 states, but releases were heavily concentrated in a handful of metro areas.

Who Took Them In?

Sponsors ranged from parents and guardians to relatives to unrelated adults. That third group grew the most over time.

Waves

The flow of children was not constant. Policy shifts, crises in Central America, and the COVID-19 pandemic created dramatic surges and drops.

Eight Years of Arrivals

Monthly releases from January 2015 through May 2023, colored by country of origin.

Despite dominating U.S. political rhetoric, Mexico accounts for a strikingly small slice. This was overwhelmingly a crisis rooted in Central America — not Mexico.

The Full Picture

Stacked together, the cumulative wave of arrivals — and the outsized role of Guatemala and Honduras — comes into focus.

The 2019 Surge

A humanitarian crisis at the border pushed monthly arrivals to record levels in mid-2019.

COVID Shutdown

In March 2020, the Trump administration invoked Title 42 — a public health statute — to rapidly expel migrants at the border without the asylum process normally required by law. Unaccompanied children were largely exempt, but widespread border closures and processing shutdowns caused releases to plummet anyway.

The 2021 Spike

As restrictions eased, releases surged past all previous records, peaking in late 2021 and early 2022.

Who Was Coming?

Roughly two-thirds of unaccompanied children were boys. But the gender ratio shifted over time.

A Shifting Balance

Shown as proportions, the growing share of girls over time becomes clear — especially after the 2021 surge.

The Sponsors

Every released child is matched with a sponsor — someone who agrees to care for them. But the data raises questions about the nature of these arrangements.

The sponsor categories reveal a gradient from close family to strangers: parents, siblings, aunts and uncles, family friends, and unrelated adults.

Overall Breakdown

The majority of children were released to Parent/Guardian sponsors, but a meaningful share went to Relative and Non-relative sponsors.

Patterns by Origin

Children from Guatemala were more likely to be released to non-parent sponsors than those from Honduras.

A Shifting Landscape

Over time, the share of non-relative sponsors grew. This is one of the patterns that raised alarms.

Gender Differences

Boys were more likely than girls to be released to non-relative sponsors.

Across Every State

The ZIP codes in the data paint a picture of where children ended up. Some areas received thousands; many received none. Explore the map below to see the geography of placement.

What the Numbers Don't Show

Behind every row in this dataset is a child who made one of the most dangerous journeys in the world. The data tells us where they ended up, but not what happened next.

Investigations by The New York Times found that many of these children were put to work in dangerous conditions: on overnight shifts, on rooftops, in slaughterhouses; often with sponsors who were not family at all.

This project uses anonymized data obtained through a Freedom of Information Act request. No individual children can be identified from this visualization.
